How deep is Lake Powell right now?

By Mike. Data checked 2026-07-17.

The water at Glen Canyon Dam is 384.6 feet deep right now, or 117.23 meters. Most pages answering this question hand you the 561 ft maximum depth the National Park Service publishes for a reservoir at full pool. When the surface sits below the full-pool line, that maximum stops describing the water actually behind the dam. So this page computes the real figure on every build from the live surface elevation at USGS gage 09379900, which reads 3523.6 feet above sea level, minus the streambed elevation at the dam. Nobody types the number.

Today's depth at Glen Canyon Dam

384.6 ft (117.23 m)

That comes to 68.56 percent of the full-pool depth. Sources for this card are USGS site 09379900 for the live elevation and the NPS Glen Canyon FAQ for the published constants, as of 2026-07-17.

The math has two inputs. Take the surface elevation, 3523.6 ft above sea level (1073.9933 m) from the gage, and subtract the streambed elevation at the dam, 3139 ft. The streambed figure is itself the difference of two published NPS numbers, the 3700 ft full-pool elevation minus the 561 ft maximum depth at the dam at full pool.

Depth varies across the lake, and this reading describes the deepest point, at the dam wall. Powell runs 186 miles at full pool, and the farther you get from the dam, the less this number applies.

Where today's level sits in the operating band

The surface is 176.4 ft below the 3700 ft full-pool line. Full pool is the high-water mark, where the reservoir would hold 27 million acre-feet.

For hydropower, the number that matters is the gap to minimum power pool. Today's reading sits 33.6 ft above that line at 3490 ft, the elevation below which Glen Canyon Dam can no longer generate hydroelectricity. Below it, generation stops.

The harder floor is dead pool at 3370 ft, the elevation below which water can no longer reach the outlet works. Today's surface holds 153.6 ft above it.

Between those two marks, one honest way to place today's level uses elevation alone. The surface has moved 53.45 percent of the way down the band that runs from full pool to dead pool. Read that as an elevation share, not a volume share. A true percent full needs the reservoir's area-capacity curve, which this page doesn't compute, so we don't publish one.

How the depth is changing

Every foot the surface moves is a foot of depth at the dam, so elevation changes read directly as depth changes.

Today the gage reads 3523.6 ft; a day earlier it read 3523.8 ft. The gap between those two readings is the day's move.

Thirty days ago the reading was 3527.2 ft, which makes the validated 30-day change -3.6 ft. A minus sign means the surface dropped over the month, a plus means it rose. Plenty of pages quote a level; few recompute a validated month-over-month delta on every build, and that is exactly what this figure is.

Today's surface also sits 3.68 ft above the record low since the reservoir first filled, 3519.92 ft on April 13, 2023, from Reclamation's daily pool-elevation archive.

Getting on the water

A depth reading at the dam can't tell you whether the ramp you're towing to actually reaches the water. Ramp status depends on where the waterline sits against that one ramp, and it shifts as the level moves. Use the level and the trend here as a starting point, then check the ramp itself with the people who run it. Levels are point-in-time readings and can lag real conditions, confirm ramp and hazard status with the lake operator before heading out.

Common questions

How deep is Lake Powell today?
384.6 ft at Glen Canyon Dam, from the live USGS gage reading of 3523.6 ft. Depth varies across the lake; the dam is the deepest point.
Why do most articles say Lake Powell is 561 feet deep?
Because the National Park Service publishes that figure as the maximum depth at Glen Canyon Dam at full pool. The number is real. It just describes a reservoir filled to 3700 ft, not the surface the gage reads today.
Where does the reading come from?
Parameter code 62614, the reservoir water-surface elevation series at USGS site 09379900, the Lake Powell gage at Glen Canyon Dam, Arizona. The value arrives in feet above datum.
What is full pool?
A surface elevation of 3700 ft above mean sea level. At that line Powell runs 186 miles long with a capacity of 27 million acre-feet.
What is minimum power pool?
3490 ft, the elevation below which Glen Canyon Dam can no longer generate hydroelectricity. Today's buffer above it is 33.6 ft.
What is dead pool?
3370 ft, the elevation below which water can no longer reach the outlet works. Today's buffer above it is 153.6 ft.

Where these numbers come from

Every figure on this page starts at USGS NWIS Instantaneous Values, site 09379900, parameter 62614, and the USGS NWIS Daily Values series for the same site serves as the cross-check. Published reference points come from the NPS Glen Canyon FAQ and Reclamation's five-year projection tables, and the record low comes from Reclamation's daily pool-elevation archive.

Code recomputes every displayed number from the validated reading at build time; none of them is typed by hand. Feed bindings are pinned in the site's sources file, so this page can't quietly pick up a different gage. I run LakeLevelNow full-time, and I pull readings from the same public USGS, CDEC, and USBR feeds the operators report to. A fetch that fails keeps the prior reading and labels it "may be delayed" instead of dropping the lake, and a reading older than the seven-day freshness window is never labeled live.

Levels are point-in-time readings and can lag real conditions, confirm ramp and hazard status with the lake operator before heading out.

Percent full is current storage divided by published full capacity, not a measure of water quality or safety.
